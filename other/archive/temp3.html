Wikipedia
The Constitution of Australia (also known as the Commonwealth Constitution) is the fundamental law that governs the political structure of Australia. It is a written constitution, that establishes the country as a federation under a constitutional monarchy governed with a parliamentary system. Its eight chapters sets down the structure and powers of the three constituent parts of the federal level of government: the Parliament, the executive government and the judicature.

The Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act of 1900 wasn't a constitution itself, but a legal act enabling the creation of the Commonwealth of Australia. It did this by formalising the union of six British colonies and attaching the separate, already-written Australian Constitution that outlined the structure and powers of the new federal government. This marked a turning point, granting permission for these former colonies to self-govern under a new national framework.

    What is a Parliamentary Government?

    A parliamentary government, also known as a parliamentary democracy, is a system where the legislative and executive branches are intertwined. Here's a breakdown of the key features:

    • Power rests with the Parliament: The Parliament is the main legislative body, responsible for making and passing laws.
    • Leader from the Majority Party: The party (or coalition of parties) with the most seats in Parliament forms the government, and their leader becomes the Prime Minister or Chancellor.
    • Executive Branch Drawn from Parliament: Government ministers are chosen from members of Parliament, giving the Parliament oversight over the government's actions.

    This fusion of powers contrasts with a presidential system, where the President is both head of state and head of government, and the legislative and executive branches are distinct.
    Here are some additional points to consider:

    • Head of State vs. Head of Government: Parliamentary systems often have a separate head of state, typically a monarch or figurehead, who represents the nation but holds little real power.
    • Accountability and Confidence: The Prime Minister and their government are accountable to the Parliament and can be removed if they lose the confidence of a majority vote. This keeps the government in check.
    • Opposition Party: The party (or parties) that don't form the government become the Opposition. Their role is to scrutinise the government's actions and propose alternative policies.

    Civil VS Criminal

    The line isn't clear but it is there between civil and criminal, the police decides and prosecutors assess and determine if the action falls under a criminal offence or civil dispute.

    Criminal Offences:
    If the act is considered a serious offence against the state (e.g., theft,
    assault, fraud), it will likely be treated as a criminal case.

    E.g Stealing a phone (punishment for breaking the law)

    Civil Disputes:
    If the act involves a disagreement or breach of contract between private parties (e.g., property damage, breach of contract), it will likely be a civil case.

    E.g Someone borrows your phone and damages it (you sue to get money to fix it)

    Civil Lawsuit Process

    1. Filing a lawsuit: The plaintiff (person bringing the case) files a claim with the appropriate court.
    2. Pre-trial procedures: Both parties may exchange evidence, participate in mediation to try to settle the case, and address any procedural matters with the judge.
    3. Trial: If no settlement is reached, a trial is held where witnesses are called, evidence is presented, and legal arguments are made.
    4. Judgement: The judge or jury delivers a verdict, deciding in favour of one party and potentially awarding remedies like damages or injunctions.
    5. Appeal: The losing party may appeal the decision to a higher court.
